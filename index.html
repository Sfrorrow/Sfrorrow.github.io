About me

Hey there! Welcome to my personal page. My name is Tommaso Proietti and I'm currently a Postdoctoral Research Fellow under Professor Conor Walsh at the Harvard Biodesign Lab at Harvard University. I received my Ph.D. in Robotics Engineering from UPMC in 2017, working at the Institute for Intelligent Systems and Robotics with Prof. Agnès Roby-Brami and Prof. Nathanaël Jarrassé. Previously, I obtained my BS and MS in Control Engineering from Sapienza University of Rome in 2010 and 2013. For my MS thesis, I also spent one full year as a Visiting Predoctoral Fellow at the Neuroscience and Robotics lab at Northwestern University, under the supervision of Prof. Kevin Lynch and Prof. Giuseppe Oriolo (from Sapienza in Rome).

Few facts about me:

Research Interests

Wearable Robotics Human-Robot Interaction Soft Robotics Translate Academic Findings

So far, I have been mostly focused on solutions to control and sense wearable devices, in both the domains of rigid and soft robotics. Wearable Robotics is a real challenge but it is undeniably our future. Robots that can help and assist people, reduce injuries, improve recovery during rehabilitation, compensate physical disability in an efficient way, or even "just" assist during fatiguing everyday tasks at home: make life easier!

There are several aspects of the Human-Robot Interaction still to address (e.g. complex mechanical coupling with the human body) but I like to focus my effort and competences on how to smartly share the control of the body between the human user and the robot, how to detect and assess the user intentions of movement, how to adapt the robot initiative to the different applications and contexts.

As any other engineers I believe, I'm very interested in solutions that can translate out of an academic environment to have an impact on the life of real people. Themes like cost-reduction (underactuation and minimal amount of sensors), robustness (control and sensing validation in unconstrained environments), proven usefulness (investigate the effects of a control strategy but also how to assess these effects) are all priorities of my research.
