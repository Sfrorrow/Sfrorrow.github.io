Dynamic Nonprehensile Manipulation

Transferring objects in the space just exploiting kinematics


Dynamic Nonprehensile Manipulation is the ability to transfer objects in the space by using a manipulator (e.g. a robotic hand) without using fingers, grippers, or any kind of tool useful to hold static equilibrium among the manipulator and objects during the full motion.

The advantage of this manipulation is that we can minimize the complexity of robot hardware and generally obtain less expensive robotic systems. Of course, the shortcoming is that modeling the dynamics of the system and utilizing contact forces to exploit dynamic effects increase exponentially the complexity of planning and control.

Nonprehensile Manipulation uses motion primitives such as rolling, sliding, pushing, throwing, etc. During the time spent at the Northwestern University for my Master thesis, we focused on a case-study of nonprehensile manipulation: we used a 3-DOF flat robotic manipulator to control a rectangular object on a frictionless plane under the effect of gravity. I dealt with defining and classifying every feasible contact combination between object and manipulator, and then used the theory of hybrid automata to model the collection of states and transitions between contact modes. The result was the generation of this complex topology.

After modeling the system, I addressed the problem of trajectory planning by designing a hierarchical two-level planner: the high-level planner, sequence planner, that manages to find a feasible sequence of contact modes as well as conditions of transition between modes, and a low-level planner, contact mode planner, that takes care of planning trajectory within single contact modes. The thesis ended with some simulations in Matlab in order to show the performance of our solution.
