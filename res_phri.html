Physical Human-Robot Interaction

Understanding the mutual adaptation between human body and robot


During my Ph.D., I studied PHRI to understand the best approach to control wearable robots, in particular when applying robotics to physical rehabilitation. While numerous robotic exoskeletons have been developed for neurorehabilitation in recent years, the few clinical results obtained by using rehabilitation robots so far did not overcome the standard care. Often, planar robots only controlling the hand movements are achieving better outcomes than multi-joint sophisticated exoskeletons.

I believe one important feature which could unleash the potentiality of rehabilitation robotics is adaptation.

During rehabilitation, the therapist naturally adapts to the patient capabilities by both varying the interaction forces with the patient's limb and by adapting the rehabilitation exercises. A key to improve the efficacy of the therapy is to learn from the therapists' example: to both adapt the assistance provided by the robot to maximize the patient engagement and physical effort, and to have the robotic therapy to evolve with the patient's recovery.

With this in mind, I aimed at better understanding the process of reciprocal adaptation (robot adapting to the human behavior, human adapting to the robot action) in a context of physical human-robot interaction. This can help developing efficient solutions to guide the robot, and, at the same time, helps understanding the way the motor behavior learns from the robot action and transfers adapted behaviors beyond the robot training.

An interesting result from my work is shown in the video. We exposed healthy individuals free motions to distributed resistive viscous force fields, generated by the exoskeleton at the joint level, to produce specific inter-joint coordination and analyzed the effects of this exposition. Clearly, we observed important inter-individual difference (adaptation to the fields in less than a quarter of the participants) but post-effects and persisting retention of these in time were observed in over 85% of the subjects, together with spatial generalization, and, preliminary transfer of the effects when moving outside of the exoskeleton.
